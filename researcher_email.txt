As federated research systems deploy AI agents to surface
patterns across distributed institution datasets, those agents
report confidence in their recommendations — not the quality
of the evidence behind them. A pattern surfaced from stale,
contradicted, or poorly-sourced data can carry an 8/10
confidence score. The researcher sees the confidence. Not
the evidence gap.

AIiTAPP is a pre-execution delegation scoring layer for
research AI agents. Before a recommendation reaches a
researcher, it scores the evidence across six dimensions —
confidence, provenance, trust history, risk, contradiction,
and data freshness — and assigns an authority state: proceed,
draft only, pause for review, or abstain. The researcher
receives an evidence packet specifying the weakest dimension
and what would need to be verified before acting.

The system does not ask whether the AI is confident.
It asks whether the evidence behind the recommendation is
good enough to act on.

This is grounded in Kahneman, Sibony, and Sunstein's noise
research — the finding that 44% variability in professional
judgment on identical cases comes not from incompetence
but from inconsistency in evidence quality at the moment
of decision. AIiTAPP applies that framework to machine agents,
where the inconsistency is in data provenance and freshness
rather than human state.

filed this month through DLA Piper. The system is running
on iOS with a server-side API deploying this week.
